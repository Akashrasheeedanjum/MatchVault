London derbies often turn on emotion. This one turned on geometry. Arsenal’s 3-1 victory over Chelsea was a clinic in positional superiority: overload one side, switch the point of attack, and punish a defensive block that arrived a half-second late too often.

Mikel Arteta’s side started with a clear plan. Declan Rice anchored, Martin Ødegaard drifted into the right half-space, and Bukayo Saka pinned Chelsea’s left flank. The first goal arrived in the 18th minute after a rehearsed sequence — a short corner recycled into the box, a flicked header cleared only to the edge, and Saka lashing a half-volley through traffic.

## Why Arsenal owned the half-spaces

Chelsea’s mid-block sat narrow in an attempt to protect the central lane for Moisés Caicedo. Arsenal simply played around it. Ben White inverted at times to create a temporary back three, freeing Ødegaard to receive on the turn. Every time Chelsea’s left-sided midfielder stepped out, Arsenal had a free man behind the press.

The second goal before half-time illustrated the pattern. A switch of play found Gabriel Martinelli isolating his full-back; the cut-back skipped the first line and Ødegaard arrived unmarked for a side-foot finish. At 2-0, the Emirates sensed control rather than chaos.

## Chelsea’s response and Arsenal’s closing statement

Cole Palmer dragged Chelsea back into the contest on 55 minutes with a trademark curled strike from the edge of the area after a turnover in midfield. For ten minutes, Chelsea looked capable of forcing a scrap. Arsenal refused the invitation.

Kai Havertz’s 71st-minute goal restored calm. A vertical pass from Rice broke two lines, Havertz held off his marker, and finished across Robert Sánchez. From there Arsenal managed tempo with patient circulation, forcing Chelsea to chase shadows in the final twenty minutes.

## Tactical lessons

Possession finished 61-39. Shots finished 19-9. Those numbers mirror what the eye test suggested: Arsenal created better angles, better rest defence, and better decisions in the final third. Chelsea were competitive in duels but disconnected between midfield and attack whenever Palmer dropped deep.

MatchVault’s verdict: this derby was won in the half-spaces. When one London side controls those pockets, the other spends the afternoon defending reactions instead of dictating them.
